H1 — Dangerous permissions correlate with lower app ratings.

Meaning:
Dangerous permissions include actions such as reading SMS, accessing location, recording audio, or modifying system settings. These permissions raise legitimate user privacy concerns.

Why This Matters:
If apps requesting more dangerous permissions receive lower ratings, it suggests that users penalize privacy-invasive behavior.

What We Tested:
We plotted the distribution of dangerous permission counts and compared it against user ratings.

Interpretation:
The plot shows that apps with high dangerous-permission counts cluster in the lower-rating region. This indicates a negative relationship between user trust and privacy invasiveness.

Conclusion:
Users appear to respond negatively to apps that request excessive sensitive permissions.

H2 — Apps with higher dangerous-permission counts are more likely to be high-risk.

Meaning: The more sensitive permissions an app requests, the higher the chance it poses a privacy threat.

Why This Matters:
Models designed for privacy-risk analysis rely on permission patterns to classify apps. If dangerous permissions strongly influence classification, it validates their usefulness.

What We Tested:
Confusion matrix from the Random Forest classifier.

Interpretation:
High-risk apps (Class 1) were predicted with significantly higher recall and precision, indicating that dangerous-permission-heavy apps create a distinct pattern for the classifier.

Conclusion:
Dangerous permission count is a strong indicator of privacy risk.

H3 — App categories differ significantly in their privacy-risk profiles.

Meaning: Different categories naturally require different levels of access. For example, Communication apps may require SMS access, while Games do not.

Why This Matters:
Risk cannot be evaluated without accounting for an app’s intended function.

What We Tested:
Boxplot of dangerous permissions across app categories.

Interpretation:
Categories like Communication, Tools, and Productivity request the highest number of sensitive permissions, while Books, Comics, and Games request very few.

Conclusion:
Privacy risk varies significantly by category, and category must be included as a predictor.

H4 — Price and privacy-risk interact.

Meaning: Free apps often rely on advertising or data collection, which may require more permissions.

Why This Matters:
A relationship between cost and permissions can highlight monetization strategies driven by user data.

What We Tested:
Feature importance from the Random Forest model.

Interpretation:
Price consistently appeared as one of the influential predictors — free apps request more dangerous permissions on average.

Conclusion:
Free apps tend to rely more heavily on data access, raising privacy concerns.

H5 — Popularity (number of ratings) is negatively correlated with dangerous permissions.

Meaning: If apps requesting many dangerous permissions receive fewer downloads, it indicates that users avoid such apps.

Why This Matters:
Popularity can be an indirect measure of user trust.

What We Tested:
Feature importance ranking, highlighting number of ratings.

Interpretation:
"Number of Ratings" scored highly in importance, suggesting that popular apps request fewer risky permissions.

Conclusion:
User behavior reinforces privacy awareness — risky apps receive lower adoption.

H6 — Specific permissions strongly predict the final risk class.

Meaning: Not all dangerous permissions carry equal weight. Some permissions are more strongly associated with risky behavior.

Why This Matters:
Identifying key permissions helps in designing lightweight privacy-screening tools.

What We Tested:
Random Forest feature importance breakdown.

Interpretation:
Permissions like:
- SMS access - Precise GPS location - Read phone identity - Modify/delete storage were among the strongest predictors.

Conclusion:
Certain permissions act as “red flags” for privacy risk classification.

H7 — A machine learning model can predict privacy risk with high accuracy.

Meaning: Automated tools could help users and researchers detect privacy-invasive applications.

Why This Matters:
Manual inspection of thousands of apps is impractical — ML scales this process.

What We Tested:
Final model accuracy and confusion matrix.

Interpretation:
The Random Forest classifier achieved ~80% accuracy and demonstrated especially strong performance in identifying high-risk apps.

Conclusion:
Machine learning is capable of reliably flagging potential privacy threats in Android apps.
